# Chief Complaint Triage Protocols — ER Nursing Reference
Source: Emergency Nursing Association (ENA) Triage Guidelines, ACEP

## Chest Pain Protocol

Initial assessment: Onset, character (sharp/pressure/burning), radiation, severity (0–10), associated symptoms (SOB, diaphoresis, nausea, palpitations).
Immediate actions if ESI 1–2: 12-lead ECG within 10 minutes. IV access. Cardiac monitor. Aspirin 325mg (if not allergic, no active bleeding). Troponin + BMP + CBC stat.
Red flags requiring ESI 2 escalation: Diaphoresis with chest pain, radiation to jaw or left arm, SBP < 90, HR > 120, SpO₂ < 94%, nitrate-unresponsive pain, new onset in patient > 40 with risk factors.
Differentiate: STEMI (ECG changes), ACS (troponin rise), PE (pleuritic, tachycardia), aortic dissection (tearing/ripping, BP differential between arms), GERD (positional, burning, post-meal).

## Shortness of Breath / Dyspnea Protocol

Initial assessment: Onset (sudden vs gradual), position of comfort, ability to speak in full sentences, accessory muscle use, cough (productive/dry), fever, leg swelling, recent travel, cardiac history.
Immediate actions if ESI 1–2: Oxygen to maintain SpO₂ > 94%. Upright position. IV access. Monitor.
Red flags: SpO₂ < 90%, inability to speak in sentences, cyanosis, tripod positioning, stridor, silent chest (no breath sounds = impending respiratory failure).
Differentiate: Asthma exacerbation (wheezing, prior history), COPD exacerbation (chronic smoker, barrel chest), CHF (bilateral crackles, orthopnea, leg edema), PE (sudden onset, pleuritic pain, tachycardia), pneumothorax (sudden onset, decreased breath sounds unilaterally), anaphylaxis (urticaria, exposure history).

## Altered Mental Status Protocol

Initial assessment: AVPU scale (Alert, Verbal, Pain, Unresponsive). GCS. Pupil response. Glucose fingerstick STAT. Last known baseline.
Immediate actions: Fingerstick glucose for ALL AMS presentations. IV access. Monitor. O₂.
AEIOU-TIPS mnemonic — causes: Alcohol, Epilepsy, Insulin (glucose), Opiates/Overdose, Uremia, Trauma, Infection, Psychiatric, Stroke/Structural.
Red flags: New focal neurological deficits, GCS < 14, fingerstick < 60 or > 400, fever + AMS = meningitis/encephalitis until ruled out, known dialysis patient, drug ingestion history.
Action: Glucose < 60 = D50 IV immediately (do not wait for labs). New focal deficits = stroke code.

## Abdominal Pain Protocol

Initial assessment: Location, character (crampy/constant/colicky), radiation, onset, bowel/bladder changes, last bowel movement, vomiting (bilious?), last menstrual period in females of childbearing age, fever.
Surgical emergencies to consider: Appendicitis (RLQ, migration from periumbilical, fever, rebound), perforated viscus (sudden severe onset, rigid abdomen, peritoneal signs), bowel obstruction (distension, colicky pain, no flatus/BM, vomiting), mesenteric ischemia (severe pain out of proportion to exam, atrial fibrillation).
Obstetric emergencies: All females of childbearing age → urine hCG stat. Ectopic pregnancy = sudden lower quadrant pain + shoulder pain + hypotension.
Red flags: Peritoneal signs (rebound, guarding, rigidity), pulsatile abdominal mass (AAA), hypotension with abdominal pain, bilious vomiting, pain out of proportion to physical exam.

## Headache Protocol

Initial assessment: Onset (thunderclap vs gradual), severity (worst headache of life?), associated symptoms (fever, stiff neck, photophobia, phonophobia, visual changes, focal neuro deficits, vomiting).
Thunderclap headache: Maximal intensity at onset or within 60 seconds = subarachnoid hemorrhage until proven otherwise. CT head stat.
Red flags (SNOOP4): Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss), Neurological signs, Onset sudden, Older patient (>50, new headache pattern), Postural component, Precipitated by Valsalva, Progressive pattern, Prior headache history change.
Meningitis triad: Fever + headache + stiff neck. Add photophobia and altered mental status = bacterial meningitis until ruled out. LP after CT (if no papilledema or focal deficits).
Action: CT head without contrast for: thunderclap, worst headache of life, fever + headache, new onset > 50, focal neurological deficit, altered mental status, anticoagulated patient.

## Syncope / Loss of Consciousness Protocol

Initial assessment: Witnessed vs unwitnessed, duration, prodrome (lightheadedness, palpitations, diaphoresis, nausea), recovery (immediate vs prolonged confusion), injury during fall, prior episodes, cardiac history, medications.
Cardiac syncope (high risk): No prodrome, during exertion, preceded by palpitations, structural heart disease, family history of sudden cardiac death, abnormal ECG.
Vasovagal (low risk): Classic prodrome, occurred in hot/crowded environment, prolonged standing, emotional trigger, rapid recovery, normal ECG.
San Francisco Syncope Rule — high risk if any of: H (history of CHF), E (hematocrit < 30%), E (abnormal ECG), S (shortness of breath), SBP < 90 on arrival.
Action: All syncope → ECG stat. Orthostatic vitals. IV access. Cardiac monitor.

## Stroke / TIA Protocol

Initial assessment: FAST screen (Face, Arm, Speech, Time). Exact time of symptom onset or last known well. Blood glucose. BP (both arms if aortic dissection concern).
Inclusion criteria for tPA: Ischemic stroke + symptom onset < 4.5 hours + no contraindications.
Contraindications to tPA: Hemorrhagic stroke, BP > 185/110 (untreated), active bleeding, recent surgery (< 14 days), prior intracranial hemorrhage, platelets < 100,000.
TIA criteria: Transient focal neurological deficit, fully resolved, no infarction on imaging. High short-term stroke risk — ABCD² score.
Action: Stroke code immediately. CT head without contrast stat. Non-contrast MRI/DWI if CT negative. Neurology bedside within 15 minutes of code activation.

## Trauma Protocol

Initial assessment: Mechanism of injury (MOI), loss of consciousness, pain location, deformity, bleeding.
Primary survey (ABCDE): Airway, Breathing, Circulation (hemorrhage control), Disability (GCS, pupils), Exposure (full body exam).
Major trauma criteria (activate trauma team): Fall > 6 meters, high-speed MVA, penetrating injury to trunk/head/neck, unstable vital signs, GCS < 14.
Hemorrhage control: Direct pressure to all external bleeding. Tourniquet for extremity hemorrhage not controlled by pressure. Pelvic binder for suspected pelvic fracture.
Spinal precautions: All trauma with mechanism for spinal injury (fall, MVA, diving) until cleared clinically or by imaging.
